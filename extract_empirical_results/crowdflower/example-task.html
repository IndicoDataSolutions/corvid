Overview

In this job, you will be presented with abstracts from scientific papers in the computer science field.  Sometimes, authors will report empirical results of their experiments as numbers in their abstracts.  

The goal is to identify whether each number in an abstract is an empirical result that is also relevant to a list of keywords.

Steps

  1. Read the abstract and the list of keywords.
  2. For every highlighted number:
    1. If it is an empirical result relevant to those keywords, toggle the checkbox for that number to be On.
    2. If it is not an empirical result and/or not relevant to those keywords, keep the checkbox toggled Off.  
    3. If you're unsure, keep the checkbox toggled Off and write down the highlighted number in the text box below

Rules & Tips

There are 3 situations to toggle a number Off:

  • It is not an empirical result:
    1. "In the year 1998"
    2. "The Mechabot2000"

  • It might be an empirical result, but not relevant to this particular paper:
    1. "The unemployment rate in America is 4%, which is motivation for why we decided to study this other thing"

  • It is an empirical result of this paper's experiments, but not given the list of keywords:
    1. "We achieved 90% accuracy on DatasetA"    &    keywords are "accuracy, DatasetB"
    2. "We achieved 90% accuracy using TechniqueA"    &    keywords are "accuracy, TechniqueB"
    3. "We achieved precision and recall of 1.0 and 1.0"    &    keywords are "f1-score"

There are some tough cases:

  • "Other people achieved 90% accuracy with TechniqueA, but we achieved 91% accuracy with a slightly-modified TechniqueA"   &   keywords are "accuracy, TechniqueA"

In this case, 91 is definitely a result, so toggled On.  But the 90 could be tough to make a decision on.  These are cases where it's fine to leave it toggled Off, and add "90" to the text field.
